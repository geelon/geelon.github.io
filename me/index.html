About Me


I am currently a Master’s student studying Machine Learning at Columbia University. In June 2017, I earned my Bachelor’s degree in Mathematics at the University of Chicago. With the knowledge and skill I’ve gained, I hope that I may contribute to human flourishing. I’m invested in not only how we create technology, but also, how we understand ourselves and our culture, so that we can use technology to make a positive impact.

What I think

I think about Complexity a lot. Most likely, I’ll never become an expert because it’s really absolutely everywhere—in math and physics, in culture and society, in life.1

You probably already know quite well that Complexity loves to masquerade as Chaos, fickle, as it eludes us like water slips through fingers. Sometimes though, when serendipity is in the air, when we peer from just the right angle, coax it with just the right words, we might finally grasp it. Then, the problem becomes tractable, the phenomenon understandable.

It’s not a question of revealing some simpler reality though, but a question of transforming our own ingrained ways of thinking. This was always a problem of representation—a matter of how we see (and following this, do) things. And when the representation and the represented resonate with amicable concordance, we call this Beauty.

I seek it, and I think many others do as well:

  • in math, what is the “natural” language that often produces the most elegant proofs?
  • in machine learning, how can we select features so that learning algorithms can discover patterns?
  • in writing, of all the possible ways to convey ourselves, which sequence of words actually contains our meaning and impact?

The dialectic between the representation (structure) and represented (data) is fundamental to all problem solving:2 although the data informs the structure, structure limits and enables what we can do with data.3

What I do

Studying complexity and solving problems go hand in hand; if a problem isn’t complex, chances are it’s already been solved.4 So, it’s unsurprising that I love to solve problems.

Of course, it’s an opportunity to test my analytic skills—a show of dominance, as it were. But much more importantly, every problem reflects of our own limitations. Recall that Complexity never pretended to be Chaos; we just failed to recognize it for what it was. Really, every problem offers a chance at growth in how we think and a chance at change in how we act. How we become.

And that’s not the royal ‘we’ I’m talking about; I mean we who are mathematicians, we who are friends to each other, we who are part of a larger community. We have a lot of problems to face; we have a lot to learn. I’m hoping that this website can help me document what I learn, maybe share some thoughts, as I strive for human flourishing.

I’ll end with my philosophy: learn from everyone, be open to change, be kind.
