Memory, Myth, and Identity

The role of memory in Latin American movements and counter-movements
Posted on March 1, 2017

The construction of a cogent worldview becomes more urgent and necessary in times when “important events seem to fall from the sky, in an unpredictable yet steady stream of happenings that shock and change our social world”; if we are unable to create a narrative, we are left with and paralyzed by “the sense of a chaotic world buffeted by accident and surprise”. In many ways, those who lived through periods of turmoil and confusion such as Argentina’s Dirty War or El Salvador’s Civil War were powerless against arbitrary violence, random disappearances, unexplainable massacres. But to build a worldview is to reclaim some of that power and reintroduce order into a chaotic world. The violence and randomness is still there, yes, but if we can understand it, we can respond. We may choose to act, to die, to kill, to rebel, to acquiesce.

Every person, generation, group has their own, what Degregori calls “cosmovision”. Despite the myriad of different cosmovisions, the individual feels certainty about their own way of understanding the world because their memory convinces them of it. From their memory of the past, they can say why things today must be as they are. But memory is not simply our past experiences; it is our past mythicized. The formation of memory gives narratives to past events and archetypes to their actors – the self included; the self especially included. Not only is memory crucial to the construction of worldviews, it is crucial to the construction of self-identity. To mythicize the self allows part of our identity to transcend the particular time and historicity of the present moment in which we’re trapped. We’re trapped, but perhaps not the ‘sacred’ or ‘Platonic’ sense of self we create; this helps us understand what Mario Villani, an ex-desaparecido, means when he said he was terrified that “I [wouldn’t] make it. But it wasn’t just to survive, but to survive as me,” even though “from the moment I was kidnapped…I believed I was condemned to die”. On one hand, the mythical self is, to us, more permanent because it transcends time, on the other, its death would be more final for the same reason.

Memory and its formation has to do with how we understand our past and ourselves, consequently informing us on how to act. Thus, memory is intimately tied with power; the power to shape our identity and culture. Just as memory allowed Leftist groups to form their identity, counterrevolutions leveraged their control over what entered the collective memory. In this essay, I will attempt to show how worldviews and identities are formed, sustained, and changed through how experiences are given a narrative in the collective memory or historical consciousness.

Collective Memory and the Formation of Identity

Truth is not the main object of memory. Indeed, to juxtapose the formation of memory with the formation of myths underlines this. Even if they are ever known, the true details and events are eventually forgotten in memory, leaving in its place a story too neat too hyperbolic to have truly happened. The story is not less important than the truth, however, for these stories in our memories inform our worldview, telling us what is possible and impossible.

In this section, I look more closely at the formation of the Agrarian League in Argentina. This was an organization created antithetically to an existing oppressive system, yet how was it possible that these organizations were formed in spite of the endemic oppression? I argue that the possibility of organization was present in the collective memory prior to organization. That is, an origin myth of organization already existed in the historical consciousness. It promised the possibility of organization; without the myth in the collective memory, even the possibility of organization wouldn’t have been possible, let alone the organization itself.

The Agrarian League in Corrientes formed in response to the exploitation of the tobacco farmers by the large landowners, and by extension, the tobacco companies. The peasants “had no ally or representation” and the “sharecroppers, tenants, and migrants [were] all wretched, unprotected by labor laws”. Despite the conditions in Corrientes for peasant farmers, before the 1960s, “the idea of organization was totally foreign here, unheard of,” said Sergio Tomasella, who later became the Secretary General of the Agrarian Leagues. Feitlowitz describes two events that “provided hope and inspiration for these peasants”. First was the Cordobazo, a strike in 1969 that occurred in Córdoba, Argentina led by the automotive workers and students in the city that “ignited a chain reaction in a number of cities around the country…, [proving] that workers could fatally destabilize a military dictatorship they rightly despised”. Second was the formation of Agrarian Leagues of cotton farmers in Chaco that eventually expelled the exploitative company Agrex. With these events in the background, it was in 1972 when the Agrarian Leagues of Corrientes was formed.

It is perhaps at face value an uninteresting argument to just claim that these two events worked their ways into the tobacco farmers’ collective memory, indicated the possibility of organizing, which ultimately allowed to successfully organize. But this hides the worldview-shaking process of “concientizaçao” or “conscientization” that the peasants must have undergone. Feitlowitz describes this as “a rigorous process of confronting, analyzing, and committing to change the structural atrocities served up as normality, order. As one’s own normality and order”. It is incredible to think that just a decade before, the idea of organizing was unthinkable, and that the worldview of the peasant farmers would be that the oppression by the large tobacco landowners and companies is a “normality” that is part of a natural and immutable order. Yet, by 1972, at least 15,000 families were registered with the Leagues, accepting of a new worldview that organization was not only possible, but that it would (and it did) ameliorate their conditions.

Shifting one’s worldview “‘was very hard,’ Sergio recounted… ‘A lot of [the peasants] simply went back into the hills, where they [hid] behind a system which held no surprises, but which was working them literally to death.’”. Feitlowitz cites Paolo Freire, a Brazilian educator, who said that “conscientization involves an excruciating moment…in those who begin to conscientize themselves…. Because conscientization demands an Easter…it demands we die to be reborn”. It involves the death of our old worldview – in how we see ourselves and act in the world – in order to make way for a new identity.

Only when we understand the difficulty of conscientization can we appreciate the importance of the mythicization of the Cordobazo and the successes of los chaqueños. It is not enough to see that others in similar situations succeeded through organization, the leap must be made (albeit not an unreasonable one here) to the belief that organizing will bring similar advances to one’s own economic situation. This ‘leap’ is the processes whereby past events become collective memory, having been mythicized, if you will, and incorporated into the cosmovision.

Positive Definition of Identity through Memory

The Argentinian rural farmers gave us an example of the role that collective memory plays in giving rise to an organization. Now, we’ll move now to the events leading to the Indian (the Zapatistas, specifically) rebellion in Chiapas in 1993 in order to see an example of where the process of creating a narrative – the mythicization – of the past helps define the identity of a revolutionary group. Here, the Indians are able to actively control a collective identity across villages by interpreting common experiences that work their way into the collective memory. The Indians in Chiapas were “the state’s poorest rural poor,” and by 1993, “some [have] suffered misery and injustice for years, others newcomers to the ordeal…[but all struggling] for ‘a life worth living’”. Here, like the peasant farmers in Argentina, the Indians experienced a “toma de conciencia,” what Womack describes as “to question received faith, wisdom, and conventions, to become conscious in a new frame of mind that people, things, qualities, conditions may not be as they had seemed or been supposed to be…It is a powerful mental and moral experience, which especially strengthens the capacity to organize”.

Womack argues that the conscientization is in large part the work of the liberation theology Bishop Ruiz and other missionaries brought to Indians. The story of Exodus, certainly an important story for the bishop and missionaries, perhaps became incorporated into the Indian’s historical consciousness as well, and in their “real lives,” they recognized Exodus, “a political story of struggle against oppression and corruption, a divine promise of liberation, a humanly organized escape…[to] the promised lands”. At risk of overattributing meaning to symbols, the myth of Exodus was conceivably an archetype that indicated to the Indians not only their ability to take control of their own narrative and escape from oppression and corruption, but also the literal promise of a better land, for the right to own land was a central issue to their vulnerability.

Regardless of the role of Exodus in their collective memory, the Indian’s conscientization was certainly largely influenced by the way they claimed their own narrative. They composed a new catechism in their own indigenous languages that framed their “struggle against poverty, hunger, and injustice” as part of the necessary struggle for land and freedom. Even more defiant, they organized an assembly of the many Indian villages in which they “recounted the misery and indignity of their lives, denounced particular injustices…, analyzed the causes of their poverty, torments, and frustration, and discussed strategies for action”. In this way, we see that the Indians are able to reclaim power over their own lives – their suffering is no longer pain that befalls them, but something they willingly take on that will eventually lead them to land and freedom. They actively shaped their collective memory in how they interpret their past experiences, and in doing so they tomaron conciencia and sustained their collective identity in organization, united by how they understood their relations with the oppressive “outside”.

Negative Definition of Identity through Memory

Through conscientization, we recognize that the world is not as we previously understood it, and as a result, we must also redefine our own roles in the world. But often, the past way of seeing the world does not stop existing; sometimes we see it in other people, and always we can find in our own historical consciousness. And so, the past worldviews continue to have power over us, even if, and especially if, we redefine ourselves as the negative of the past. We can look to Peru and the formation of identity amongst the Sendero Luminoso, the “Shining Path”, both as an example as a way of clarification.

In the 1970s, “for any Peruvian leftist who was more-or-less informed, ‘Shining Path’ was simply the name of one of the factions that called themselves a version of the Peruvian Communist Party or perhaps constituted one of the university groups”. At first, Shining Path attracted many “provincial students” seeking “organizations that provided them with group identities, integrated them into a new environment, and permitted them to get through their university studies without major setback”. Yet, by May 1981, “ideological zeal, military strategy, and totalitarian political project came together” and the leader, Abimael Guzmán, “propagated the idea of ‘the blood quota’ that would be necessary for the triumph of the revolution, and warned…[that] the militants had to be ready to ford ‘the river of blood’…while ‘carrying their lives on their fingertips’”. How did Shining Path transform from a relatively unknown university organization into a group whose cadres were “ideologized to the point of fundamentalism, ready to kill and die for their project”? That is, how did Shining Path become such a “total identity”? Part of the answer, I claim, lies in their rejection of other identities within their collective memory or historical consciousness. Their search for their own identity apart from similar Leftist groups pushed them to be more radical, and their excessive violence was also perhaps more symbolic or religious than pragmatic to affirm their break from the past.

Through the late 60s and early 70s, Peru was controlled by a “military regime that seemed to preempt the Left” in that it “undertook reform…including agrarian reform and nationalization of natural resources. And, the military government dealt blows to the Left’s great enemies: the oligarchy and APRA”. It was a “paradoxical situation” that caused the Left to have a “constant preoccupation with differentiating the Left from the regime” that also led to Left having “scant clarity – in other words, [it was unable] to establish a clear, public difference that offered the country a viable alternative to authoritarianism and savagery”. With this in the background and collective memory, the Shining Path and its students began to define their own identity in contrast to it; the “presence of other parties of the Left…also encouraged youthful radicalism”. Furthermore, the youths themselves were also “in search of an identity; their parents’ ‘traditional’ Andean identity seemed remote after exposure to the ‘myth of progress’”. This ‘myth of progress’ promoted by the schools, mass media, and “even by their own parents” promised the possibility of progress, yet there was “little hope of achieving such progress by way of the market, migration, or more education”. To the students, joining the Shining Path was seen as a “concrete possibility of social assent…a path for social mobility”. So, once again, like with the Argentinian peasant farmers, organization follows from the myth of progress having been incorporated into the students’ collective memory; the myth is not just a story, it is a promise of real power and real change. But here, we see how collective memory further defines the Shining Path’s identity in negation of the existing military regime, of the existing Left, and of the students’ own past Andean culture.

This set the stage for the violence in Shining Path’s maturity. As a symbolic departure from the Andean peasant notion of “punish but don’t kill” as a form of justice, the Shining Path tells the peasant community, “Oh, you all still have your archaic ideas…We have to cut off [the guilty’s] heads…because if we are going to be forgiving the bad weed we are never going to triumph, we are never going to exceed ourselves (superarnos)”. Thus, the violence is not pragmatic, but symbolic, underlining the youths’ “anxiousness” and need to overcome the past “Andean rationality”.

We have now seen how the way in which past experiences and narratives enter the collective memory play an important role in changing what sort of collective identities are formed, shaped, and sustained. As Patricio Guzmán said in his film Nostalgia for the Light, “la memoria tiene fuerza de gravedad. Siempre nos atrae.” But perhaps memory’s most important role is in shaping what is possible to us, in allowing us to believe we can shape the present. Guzmán continues: “Los que tienen memoria son capaces de vivir en el frágil tiempo presente. Los que no la tienen no viven en ninguna parte”. It’s clear that memory plays a powerful role in the formation and continuation of revolutionary movements especially on the Left. However, memory may also be manipulated and suppressed by counterrevolutionary forces; we’ll look again to Argentina’s Dirty War to consider how and why they controlled the formation of memory.

Counter-myth and Paralysis

At the beginning of this essay, I described how we can think of memory formation as the mythicization of past events, a way of giving our world and lives a narrative that both informs and supports our worldview. But this process is not immune to interference from the outside. Indeed, Admiral Emilio Massera, one of the, if not the mastermind of Argentina’s Dirty War, argues that “[reality] is a veritable world war whose battlefield is the human spirit”, where real powers in the world struggle to control the way we think about the world; this is a war in which “even the Word of God is used by murderers to invent a theology that justifies violence”. For Massera, interference is not only possible, but necessary, to prevent the individual’s mind to be corrupted by “murderers” justifying violence. For Massera, language shapes the way the events are mythicized or narrativized. For the individual, this narrativization can be empowering when attaining a cogent worldview allows the individual certainty in their actions. Massera and the military regime realize the converse – if they can confuse the Argentinians by building dissonance into their worldviews, the Argentinians will be unempowered, paralyzed, and unable to organize or act. This is their insidious goal.

It worked too. “The official rhetoric of the Dirty War drew much of its power from being at once ‘comprehensible,’ incongruous, and disorienting”. A mother of a desaparecido said that “it made you psychotic. We could barely ‘read,’ let alone ‘translate’ the world around us. And that was exactly what they wanted”. By creating a world full of cognitive dissonance, individuals could not form a certain worldview. For example, in rural Argentina, the regime made efforts to assist the peasants by bringing in doctors, agronomists, engineers, and more education. However, “these efforts toward ‘progress’ were intended not to educate, but to confuse; not to enable, but to paralyze. The campaign of terror coexisted with programs for assistance”. The identities of benefactors and enemies were inseparable, the actions of the regime both helped and hurt the peasants. In this way, the regime was able to keep the collective memory incoherent and confused. This kept the peasants without a clear foe in their historical consciousness to rally and organize against.

Conflicting information wasn’t the only way the regime controlled the collective memory. They created their own myths and disseminated false information. As Feitlowitz describes Massera’s speech, Dead for the Fatherland, the speech was “a dramatic weaving of myth, magic, lyricism, and lies, delivered like a sermon. It offers a history lesson in the form of a fairy tale”. That the speech is myth-like or sermon-like is just to recognize that the speech was explicitly attempting to control the way people thought, their worldviews. However, the propaganda was everywhere. From advertisements in the New York Times Magazine abroad to whole magazines circulated at home, the image and message of the regime was always in conflict to their reality to great success. As the child now grown up said, “You know the worst thing about this? I believed every word; my mother was a faithful reader”.

The regime’s attack on the collective memory led to a youth without a collective identity; there was no way for the youth to rise up against the regime. As Miguel Bonasso wrote in De los “desaparecidos” a los “chicos de la guerra” in Nueva sociedad in 1985, the youth of Argentina “se asomaban a su primera juventud”; bred by the Process to be apolitical, “desinformados y deformados por el terror”, they “vivían en la total despolitización”. Certainly, we shouldn’t attribute the depoliticization of the youth only to the regime’s attack on their collective memory, but on the other hand, there is a difference between being unwilling to act and being unawares of the possibility to act; the latter is a result of a much more insidious oppression that left the youth ignorant to their own oppression.

The Persistence of Memory

Part of what makes memory so influential is that the power it bestows into our worldviews last; it’s not clear that even immanently changing a worldview is easy. The persistence of the many atrocities and struggles throughout Latin America remains in memory, and decades later, the worldviews informed by these memories still have power, affecting what is believed to be possible and impossible.

There are many cases where extreme oppression and violence has created a culture of passivity and silence. Feitlowitz writes: “in [those] in whom passivity was ingrained through terror, it will not encourage free inquiry or reflection on the past”. This persistence of memory and terror is seen throughout our readings for this class. For example, in 1931, the El Salvadoran general Maximiliano Martínez committed a “campaign of repression so ferocious that it came to be known simply as la matanza,” yet “a half-century later, when [rebellion] raged once again in Salvador, those areas where the killing had been rampant five decades before remained remarkably quiescent”.

We can consider the Yarur textile factories in Chile. In the 1970s, the factories had two cohorts of workers: the “Old-timers” who were “all survivors…of Yarur’s repressive paternalism, of the purges that had followed the failure of previous workers’ movements”, and the “Youngsters”, a “new generation of workers – more educated and confident, class-conscious and politically aware” that had also “never experienced a traumatic defeat at the hands of the Yarurs”. Here, change had to be driven by the Youngsters. For the Old-timers, “the problem was to convince them that things could change” not just that they should work toward change. The past defeat and repression of the Old-timers had fundamentally changed what they saw possible.

As another example, we can look to rural Argentina again, and the Agrarian Leagues of Corrientes. We left their story just after the Agrarian Leagues were formed. We return in the aftermath of the repression by the Process. The League is destroyed, the military regime is condemned. Yet, the fear remains. “It isn’t only that people won’t talk about these things, they are afraid to hear about them” an interviewee told Feitlowitz. In fact, “what happened has not been passed down from elders to the youths. Fathers haven’t told their sons, uncles haven’t told their nephews. So the facts and events aren’t known to the rising generation”. Not just is the history missing from the collective memory, “peasants here today have no organization of any kind that exists to represent their interests. The Agrarian Leagues were the first, as well as the last, attempt by the peasants to create such a body for themselves”.

What all of these examples suggest is that even though they many cycles of revolution and counterrevolution may be over now in places such as Argentina or Chile. However, we can’t expect that their memory and power is also gone. Sometimes, the power is still frighteningly real. Consider that in 2006, two decades after the end of the Process, Jorge Julio López was disappeared “soon after he testified [in the trial of Etchecolatz],” who had run eight concentration camps . That power can continue to remain real; without understanding how these atrocities came to pass, without condemning the violence, we allow in our collective memory the myth that terrible oppression can go unexplained and unpunished.

Where does this leave us then? If I am honest, I have just mythicized important parts of history in Latin America. I have woven the events together under a narrative, hopeful that there are lessons to be learned from even the worst of violence. I must and want to end hopefully: that it is possible for us to take an active role in building our collective memory and defining our own identities. There is also, of course, more real reasons to be hopeful for the future of Latin America. All the texts we read in this course hopes to reclaim the narrative, how history is understood, and how it may be redeemed. The Tribunals of Impunity seek to incorporate justice into institutional memory, as an active way of shaping the future. And of course, Latin America, and more broadly, the world, is still here; we have another chance.
