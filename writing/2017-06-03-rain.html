The Possibility of Overcoming Violence

Understanding violence as a phenomenal form
Posted on June 3, 2017

The violence captured in photos is like the words spoken in another language. Incomprehensible and barbaric, though still human. And when our minds fail to supply the words with meaning, it falls to our senses to experience them—just sound waves beating at our eardrums. Violence felt through voyeurism is just as sensuous. When our experience of violence isn’t mediated through ourselves, but through images, movies, and texts, it’s particularly easy to see violence as it physically is—just a human killing another. So, it is hard to comprehend the honor in honor killing. And even the killing itself is hard to understand.

Instead, we imagine ourselves as the victims, and we shudder at the destruction of our bodies. We imagine ourselves as their families, and we hope to never have to share their emotional trauma. Finally, we imagine ourselves holding the gun, inflicting the violence. But we can’t shoot. How could we, when we remember how it feels? We wonder how could they pull the trigger? What implacable anger overpowered any sense of empathy to see what suffering they would cause? If they could’ve just realized that violence begets only violence, killing leads to more killings, and in the end, nothing is solved. It could have happened differently.

Viewed as such, images of trauma serve to remind us of the senselessness of violence. Viewed from afar, the history of violence easily becomes a history of apologies to the victims who suffered meaningless and avoidable deaths. We think that violence is the problem—violence, which is borne in those who have become too blinded by emotion and ideology; violence, whose sinister face tricks us into thinking that we just need to, as Anne tells Aleksander, take sides against war. But in reality, the problem runs deeper, for violence is merely a phenomenal form, a symptom of an underlying sickness.

Like sounds of foreign words or flashes of lightning, violence gives sensuous form to the processes that produced it. And so, it is inconsequential to take sides against war itself. Indeed, Aleksander callously answers Anne, like it matters. What does it matter if we’re against violence? Does it make a difference if we’re against lightning? Lightning is just the messenger who exposes the deception of still thunderclouds. There, chaotic collisions of ice crystals generate a charge, building and building, before breaking across the sky to finally betray the tumult within. And when violence erupts, it reveals not itself, but the ripening of the very conditions for violence—and that? Well, that has been happening yesterday, and the day before, and the day before that.

Not only does it not matter if we ‘take sides against war’, but by fixating on the form of violence, we can’t move beyond the alienation of it, and it seems that violence is something wholly independent from us. As a result, we become limited in two ways.

First, if we think that violence has nothing to do with us, we must also think ourselves powerless to overcome it—like news on the television: we can only watch helplessly. But as with any foreign language, we shouldn’t fixate on how exotic it sounds; instead, we should ask ourselves, why do the both of us have words to mean the same thing? Thus, why might we all produce violence? Before, we had called those killers blind—perhaps because of emotion or ideology—but what about us? Are we blindly empowering the production of violence, even if violence hasn’t manifested itself in its familiarly vicious form?

Second, if we believe ourselves to be alienated from violence, we allow ourselves to exoticize others as “a violent people.” We might say to them, if you just stop being violent, society will be better. But violence is a symptom, like the cough of a patient. Perhaps it’s equally ridiculous as saying to the patient, if you just stop coughing, you’ll feel better. However, what’s worse than misunderstanding violence is basing an identity on the phenomenal form of violence. We think that because we could never be as violent as they are, they must have some quality that inherently sets them apart from us. That is, nonviolence is a part of our identity, and violence is a part of theirs. But then, to oppose violence, saying, those people ought not to be violent, is to act on an imagined quality we impose onto those Others. If we were to believe this and if we were to say this, might this not lead to more conflict? And this time, we’ve surely participated in producing the violence ourselves.

We must try to understand violence, certainly not to justify it, but to try to reflect upon the reasons we might produce it. It is in hopes that in understanding this, we may be able to end current cycles of violence. We may be able to overcome our natural reflexes, despite conditions that dispose us, even if unknowingly, toward violence. This way, the history of violence may no longer be one of apologies—apologies that shamefully admit to the dead that we are unable to escape human cruelty—but one of redemption. That, although we can no longer do anything for its passed victims, we’ve learned from their trauma, and because of their suffering and sacrifice, we’ve freed ourselves from violence.

Violence as Intrusion

The first episode of violence in Before the Rain happens on a hilly countryside of Macedonia. Till now, only a subtle light bulb suggests we must be nearing modern day, but it is not until we’re affronted by a band of men carrying automatic rifles, wearing graphic tees, and listening to Walkmans do we realize that it is modern day.

For any viewer accustomed to airplanes, high-rise office buildings, the genteel restaurants of a metropolis like London, this land feels very alien. We watch as a young girl scrambles and hides in unfamiliar territory, evading the band of armed men accusing her of murder. Her distress infects us, insidious. It’s only when we’re safely past the denouement and some ways into the next episode that our anxiety drains away. Was it really there the whole time? We can’t remember. But now, we’re in London. Home, and at ease. And the world is as it ought to be.

We look on as Anne examines photos documenting the violence from the first episode. She’s peering at the same young girl through a magnifying glass, all the while chatting on the phone. It’s still sad, but this time, danger doesn’t press up against us. In here, Order is in charge, and there’s no place for Chaos.

But Chaos intrudes anyways, catches us in our complacency. We’re shocked to discover that the glass between us and violence, which we thought would keep us safe, was never there. The conflict seeps out of Anne’s photographs, and this time, she experiences it much more personally when in its aftermath, her husband lies dead and bloodied on the floor of a restaurant.

Violence takes the same form in both the photo and the restaurant—the girl and the husband shot dead. In both, violence is the unwanted intrusion of the shooters’ power into their victims’ lives. However, Anne certainly experiences them differently. With the girl, Anne is just an outside observer. But when her husband is shot during their dinner together, she is surely also a victim of violence, despite being unhurt. Is the difference just that she has emotional ties to her husband and not the girl from the pictures? Perhaps. But suppose her husband had traveled to Macedonia instead and gotten shot there. Is she still a victim of violence? Though she’d probably experience sadness all the same, but she wouldn’t feel as though she were a victim. And in fact, when in the film, her lover Aleksander is shot in Macedonia and we see his funeral, we see a distant apparition of Anne as well. She’s somber, yet compared to the villagers’ vocal grief, stoic. No one imagines her a victim there. But then, how is Anne a victim in the restaurant? Why is violence at home so much more potent than abroad?

In the photos, violence is strange, but there, the land is strange too. It arouses our curiosities if not our sympathies. At home, though, violence speaks our own language and so it is we who give it that potency. Even as we produce the meaning of words we know, we actively produce our experience of violence. When the shooter encroaches onto the life of Anne’s husband, he also invades into Anne’s sense of Order in the world, her sense of how the world ought to be.

Here, ought is not in the conscious, premeditated sense of how we think the world should be. Instead, it reflects our unconscious desires for how we believe the world will continue to be, for we have no way to know whether tomorrow will turn the world upside-down. All we have is the knowledge that day after day, some things in our lives have remained constant. Repetition teaches that if I go to school, I will learn. If I go to a restaurant, I can eat. If I follow the law, I can live my life as I wish. No one will break down my front door. No one will punch me on the street. So, while our minds slowly discovers Order in the world, our bodies perform everyday actions as the rituals that reaffirm and rebuild that very Order. Empowered, it tells us that if we proceed by its rules, all will be well tomorrow too. We follow Order because it promises us what we can never have: certainty.

So when the shooter enters the restaurant with a gun and shoots Anne’s husband, he forces Anne to confront the truth. That the sacred line between Order and Chaos was an imagined one, hallucinated by her own unconscious desires. It turns out that it was only chance and folly that had kept Chaos at bay. But Chaos intrudes anyways, and we call this Violence. Ugly evidence of a broken promise, of when the past failed to become what it should have been.

But remember that Order, and thus Chaos as well, were produced from our own minds. So, Violence is also an artifact of our own subjective experience of reality in relation to what ought to be. That is, when this intrusion as a process occurs, we experience Violence; we are the victim to those who were violent. But when we observe violence, we ourselves might not have to experience the world-shaking process of Violence. The observed violence can remind us of a time when we ourselves were victimized, perhaps providing objective proof that our Order is not inviolable. But, Violence only occurs when there is an exercise of power that forces us to confront the truth. That force doesn’t exist through the medium of photographs or movies or text, for when the truth becomes too much to bear, we can just look away and reject the reality of the depicted violence.

It is in this way that violence experienced through voyeurism is just a phenomenal form. It calls back to the shape Violence took in that moment in mythical time when we fell victim. So, when things reconstruct that same form, as though in ritual repetition of the primordial Violence, we supply the violent meaning ourselves, and we say that those things appear violent to us.

Voyeurism is not the only way through which things may appear violent without having the Order-reshaping content of Violence. The lion that rips apart its prey is not Violent because it follows the natural order of things. The executioner who administers the lethal injection to the serial killer is not Violent because this is what law and order of society demands. Even if natural disasters violently end many innocent lives, they’re not Violent as murder is precisely because they are natural. Although each of these reminisce of violence, and perhaps we think that they ought not happen, we believe that they are part of how the world works, so they do not force us to confront the Real of our unconscious desires.

In summary, when things appear violent to us, it is because they happen to repeat in the image of Violence as Violence showed itself to us. Consequently, we, as the subjective viewer of violence, must not end our experience with the alienation of ourselves from that original Violence. Otherwise, if we do forget that Violence is birthed from our own unconscious desires, it would seem to us that those Others who participate in violence are imbued with some “fascinating secret” or “fantasy-object”, like an exotic beast. As Zizek writes, in reality, it is the “kernel of [our] own enjoyment” that produced our own fascination (Zizek, 69).

***

Now that we understand better what Violence is and our own roles in producing our experience of it, we must ask how can we use this knowledge free ourselves from violence. We come to quite a conundrum: must we experience Violence in order to overcome it? Surely, not, for this suggests that we can learn nothing by watching films or studying photographs of violence. Thus, we must somehow alienate Violence in the sense that we can recognize it without having to experience the process of Violence directly. On the other hand, we must be cautious in how we alienate Violence, for we just saw how easily we may alienate and exoticize Others in the process. And this only continues the vicious cycles of violence.

It seems to me that there are two different approaches. First, we realize that our own experience of Violence is produced by our own desires. If we may control our desires, it seems then that we can control our experience of Violence. However, this banishes the self to live in the chaotic; wherever the currents take us, we’ll go, and we’ll retroactively say that’s where we wanted to end up. Perhaps it’s true that all we can do is internalize Violence and rid ourselves of desire. However, there may be another way.

We must alienate Violence to the extent we can recognize our own violence. This is the only way we can identify ourselves with our victims. For the Violence that I produce in others is within my power to change. Now, the pressing questions at hand: how can we recognize the Violence we produce? What forces us to produce it?

If we can overcome this, maybe we can overcome Violence itself.
