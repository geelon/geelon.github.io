Sacrifice in Time of Parting [unfinished]

The role of trauma and violence in forming identity and ideology
Posted on May 23, 2017

Anton donchev’s novel, Time of Parting, is a story of the forced and violent conversion of Christian villagers living in the Rhodope mountains to Islam. The villagers are faced with a question: does one die for their Christian faith? Or does one convert, dying metaphorically perhaps, by rejecting an integral part of both one’s own identity and way of connection to the community? Near the close of the novel, one of the villagers, the priest, experiences a revelation – the ‘fundamental essence’ of the villagers was not that they were Christian, but that they were Bulgarians. So, converting isn’t a betrayal one’s sublime identity. Because of this realization although many do die, many also convert and live.

In the end, it appears that the Turks, the proselytizers, have succeeded. The countryside seems full of Muslims; those who have converted donning the turban and veil, and those who refused no longer living. But theirs is just a formal victory. The Turks fail to realize that forced conversion vulgarizes the religious categories for the villagers. If we call the sublime identity that “other ‘indestructible and immutable’ body which persists beyond the corruption of the physical body,” then religious identity, by definition, can no longer be sublime (Zizek, p. 12). Instead, like the material body, it constitutes a part of the vulgar identity. Chop off my arm; my “body within the body” remains intact. Change my religion; my essential identity is unadulterated. The Turks have failed because the countryside isn’t actually full of Muslims; it’s full of Bulgarians.

In short, the conversion was religious in form, but nationalistic in content. Through trauma, the villagers discovered their national identity, which appeared as more essential than their religion. However, this revelation produces a paradox. It seems then, that either the martyrs died in vain when they could have just converted, or the converted betrayed those who did die for their religion. Yet, the novel suggests neither is true. How can this be the case?

As an event in the novel, their martyrdom was a literary necessity. A story where the villagers immediately acquiesces to the demands of the Turks would have been unconvincing and unsatisfying to the reader. On the other hand, the novel could not have ended with everyone’s death. Then, the reader would just be left with an overwhelming sense of nihilism and powerlessness under Chaos’ reign of senseless violence. The readers must suffer with the villagers, but they must also be allowed to reclaim their suffering as sacrifice – suffering that is made sense and given meaning through ideology. Thus, the implied reader’s experience parallels the conversion of the villagers in the novel. It is this relation between suffering and ideology that we’ll explore in the rest of this essay. I will use Time of Parting to understand four points of The Sublime Object of Ideology, by Slavoj Zizek.

  1. Identity is an expression of potentiality rather than actuality – it relates us to what we could have been, and what we want to be.
  2. Violence/trauma produces and sustains the ideology that allows us to bear the trauma of reality. In short, we believe that our ideology has a divine or mythical quality, and so, the parts of our identity supported by ideology – the sublime identity – becomes perceived as impervious to the original violence. However, the ‘radical contingency’ of the original violence must be ‘repressed’ in order for the ideology to have power.
  3. There is an ‘excess’ of trauma that escapes symbolization by the ideology, reminding us of the radical contingency. It manifests itself as a ‘symptom’ of the ideology. Dialectically, when faced with the contradictions of our ideology, we change.
  4. The conclusion of this dialectical process is the ‘externalization’ of our identity in the sense that we no longer find it necessary to control it, nor does it control us. As Zizek writes, “one really reconciles oneself with some objective content not when one still has to strive to master and control it, but when one can afford the supreme sovereign gesture of releasing this content from oneself, of setting it free” (Zizek, p. xxi).

Brotherhood: An Identity

An individual’s identity exists only in relation to others. If I say that I am a brother, then I draw on the identity that other brothers share. Zizek offers an anti-descriptivist formulation for how the relational symbol brother obtains its meaning: there is a “primordial baptism” at a certain point in “prehistory” in which the certain kind of person we want to call a brother was baptized with the identity brother (Zizek, p. 99). This baptism aims the symbol at what Zizek variously calls the rigid designator, the surplus-object, the Lacanian objet petit a, or the Real of one’s desires. Each of these gets at the sublime or Platonic form of brother, or that which is “in an object more than the object” (Zizek, p. 107).

To clarify, let’s consider the meaning brother starts off with: the notion of sharing the same parents. But in Time of Parting, we can infer that brother has obtained a more abstracted meaning of sharing the same roots along the sanctity of that bond; one could never harm their brother. The narrator Abdullah shares an epiphany of what brotherhood meant to the villagers: “I realized that it all had to do with brotherhood. These men were brothers of the men in the valley and of the men in all the Bulgarian lands. They wanted to remain their brothers, and did not want to raise a hand against them” (Donchev, p. 254). Even for Karaibrahim, the anti-hero, brotherhood implied a sacred bond. At the beginning of the novel, he contemplates shooting a distant cowherd who refused to be summoned, but restrains himself. Karaibrahim doesn’t answer the hodja’s question “why didn’t you shoot, Aga?” (Donchev, p. 14). But for fear of shooting his own brother, it is implied. This worsens the tragic moment of realization when his brother tried to kill him. He laments: “Why didn’t my brother realize that I was his brother? Why didn’t his blood speak?” (Donchev, p. 252).

Thus, brother is someone with whom you had a sacred bond. Perhaps familial ties or national ties. He could be a comrade-in-arms. He’s one would not betray you, nor could you betray him. However, this cannot fully encapsulate what brother means – there is something more sublime or Platonic that I miss in defining a brother in this way. That is precisely the surplus-object of desire that Zizek describes. This rigid designator is the part of the meaning of a name or identity that eludes symbolic description.

Zizek remarks that “the crucial point to grasp is the connection between the radical contingency of naming and the logic of emergence of the ‘rigid designator’ through which a given object achieves its identity” (Zizek, p. 107). That is, to understand where the rigid designator arises, recall that our notion of brother came from our own expectations or desires for what brother is. We said that he is ‘the certain kind of person we want to call a brother’.

Our identity is therefore a multiplicity of ways we relate to others. If I say that I am a brother, then I have a desire or expectation for myself to protect and never harm my siblings. If I say that I am a leader, then I expect that I be a shepherd of the people. In other words, our identity lays claim to our potential, how we should act and what we should become.
