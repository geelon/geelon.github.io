Our worldview as religion

Posted on February 11, 2017

Asad critiques Geertz’s “universal definition of religion” in The construction of religion as an anthropological category, arguing that there cannot be one: “not only because its constituent elements and relationships are historically specific, but because that definition is itself the historical product of discursive processes” (Asad 29). That is, the language we use to describe others arises from our own experiences and worldviews, so it is both insufficient in representing the contents of another’s ‘religion’ and presumptuous when it carries along with it preexisting (and external) connotations. The failure to fully understand medieval Christianity’s role in the state gives an example of the insufficiency of a universal language of religion. The paradigm-shifting effects of applying the European language of religion to Pueblo Indian culture gives an example of the latter problem.

We should recognize that, as an anthropological category (the purpose of which is to describe and understand other peoples), a “transhistorical” or “universal” language of religion has been problematic. However, if Asad criticizes Geertz for failing to recognize the limits of describing others within his own worldview, Asad himself does not escape this critique. Implicitly, Asad suggests that we achieve greater objectivity by re-contextualizing others’ religions in history: “the possibility and the authoritative status [of religious practices and utterances] are to be explained as products of historically distinctive disciplines and forces” (Asad 54). However, the notion of objectivity (or authoritativeness) is one steeped in a worldview relying on reason – a worldview in which we, Geertz, and Asad all share. To support this, we can consider Geertz’ definition of religion (ironically, it may be better studied as an anthropological object than applied as an anthropological tool). The goal then should not be, as Asad seems to indicate, to transcend subjectivity, but to realize limitations of our own worldview (a ‘religion’ of rationality).

I’ll structure this essay based on Geertz’s language in Religion as a cultural system. I am less concerned with what Geertz’s writing meant to himself than with what it may mean to a reader now, such as Asad. I view language as initially only a coarse guide to understanding.

Language, Categories, and Objectivity

Geertz’s description of a “system of symbols” serves as a useful starting point when thinking about how language is formed and used, in addition to what purpose language serves. For Geertz, symbols play a dual role, both as a model of and a model for reality. We attempt to form a language that reflects the natural structure of the world; the world is a priori a formless content – the forms, generalizations, abstractions are relations between the particulars (the relations are ones we ourselves create and use, as in language). Here, particulars become instantiations of the broader conception of the symbol. As such, symbols or language is a model of reality, the success of which we may judge through the “perception of the structural congruence” between reality and the model (Geertz 94). The forms are created for and through human understanding, so by ‘a priori’, I mean in the absence of human reason; without it, the particulars of the world remain unconcerned about forms – whether or not categories that relate them to each other are ‘real’.

Wittgenstein gives us a good example. Consider an interaction: “if you say: ‘He is dead’ and I say: ‘He is not dead’ no-one would say: ‘Do they mean the same thing by “dead”?’” (Wittgenstein 62). We perceive the states of being alive and being dead as different and generalizable enough to give them names. That no one would ask “do they mean the same thing by ‘dead’” is an indication of the structural congruence of the generalized notion of death and the particular instantiations of death. But still, the category of death remains within human understanding, for, to put it crudely, once you’re dead, you don’t care if you’re ‘dead’.

In this way, language grows out of the content we believe is “really real”, as Geertz puts it, and it tries to make explicit the ‘natural’ structure of the really real. Symbols in the model of sense relies on the assumption that the particulars can be universalized: that we think of a natural structure indicates our belief that there is an objectivity that we should strive to achieve. Here, natural means that the structure arises from reality itself, and not one imposed by us. In other words, we believe that objective categories exist, part of what Geertz might call an “envisaged cosmic order” (Geertz 90).1 And even though we may never know whether our defined categories ‘really’ manage to reflect the objective ones because we are ultimately entrenched in our own subjectivity. The most we can ask for is the perception of structural congruence.

Objectification and Power

The impossibility of reconciling objective categories and defined categories is instantiated in anyone’s attempt to define religion. This is part of Asad’s critique of Geertz’s definition to understand the relationship between power and religion in the Middle Ages: the “separation of religion from power is a modern Western norm, a product of a unique post-Reformation history” (Asad 28). Thus, Geertz’s definition of religion is a product of his own historical context. But Geertz might not disagree with this insight – for him, definitions are not the real thing, but just a map or guide.

He writes “although it is notorious that definitions establish nothing” (that is, by itself, a definition is just a syntactic relationship between other elements of a language), “if they are carefully enough constructed, [definitions] provide a…way of developing and controlling a novel line of inquiry” (Geertz 90). This is to say that definitions aren’t created for the purpose of replicating ‘the natural structure’ of reality; they are created to be used.

Put another way, language is a priori a contentless form. On their own, definitions establish nothing because they are just an algebra of symbols. The content or meaning is provided by human understanding. This implies two points: (1) symbols have role as what Geertz calls a model for reality, and (2) the historicity of the definition and the immediacy its use should be recognized upon its formation. I will return to the second point in the last section of the essay.

On the first point, symbols as a model for reality underline the individuals’ relation to reality: both how they act within and how they act upon reality. Symbols change how the individual understands reality – the way the individual experiences the “really real” is changed by the symbols and language he uses to describe it. The Progressives within the Pueblo Indians are an example of how using the Protestant language of religion altered the worldview of the Puebloans: their use of the nonnative ideas of religion realized the possibility of not participating in ‘religious ceremonies’ (realization in the sense that it became real in the Progressives’ minds, not in the sense that the possibility existed before but they just hadn’t noticed it). This shift in how reality is understood is reflected back into language, in which “the language of religion and the sacred has become…indigenous ones” (Wenger 265).

The use of symbols as a model for reality is then related to the objectification of reality. Let me expand on this unfortunate sentence. We think of reality as something external from the individual – it has an objective existence (in the sense that it does not depend on its relation to the individual). Because of this, reality is imposed on the individual because the individual cannot control how the external acts on him. What I, as an individual, recognize is this: that which acts upon me has the power to act upon me.2 Paradoxically, for the individual, reality is, a priori, that which has the power to act upon the individual; however, reality as experienced by the individual is not this a priori-reality I just described. The individual’s reality is the a priori-reality objectified. The symbols I use to describe reality also localizes this power to act upon me to the objects they describe.

An example here is in order. Suppose I am sitting on a chair. Then, that chair is part of reality for me, certainly since I am sitting on it. And for me to say I am sitting on a chair, I have to have objectified the chair, and confer to it my belief that it exists even without its relation to me. In doing so, I ascribe it a power that on one hand I believe is real (real enough for me to try sitting on it), but on the other hand is also exterior to the chair itself (it doesn’t care that I understand it as a chair or that I believe it has power in relation to me). So, my reality, which includes this chair, has power over me, but the way I explain it has to do with me.

This idea is an important component to how Geertz understands religion: the “group ethos…objectivizes moral and aesthetic preferences by depicting them as the imposed conditions of life implicit in a world with a particular structure as mere common sense given the unalterable shape of reality” (Geertz 90). While he speaks in the context of religion, we may generalize them. The “imposed conditions of life” correspond to its quality of being external to the individual, and so objectivized. They are then incorporated into “common sense” – that is, my worldview or my relation to reality. And they are “unalterable” because their existence is independent from me.

Religion or Worldviews

Having set up a cursory theory on the modern-day worldview – how we come to perceive what is real, how we use symbols to understand the real, and also how we rely on a basic assumption that objective categories exist even if they are unknowable, we can return to Geertz’s definition. And here’s the punchline: Geertz’s definition of religion can be applied to our worldview in a structurally congruent way. Geertz states that a religion is:

(1) a system of symbols which acts to (2) establish powerful, pervasive, and long-lasting moods and motivations in men by (3) formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and (4) clothing these conceptions with such an aura of factuality that (5) the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic.

The categories I naturally use almost without thought, such as worldviews, to analyze Geertz’s own arguments, the language I use, and so on are part of the system of symbols I rely on. The categories that Geertz, Asad, I, and probably most modern people use seem to be based off of the desire to universalize or generalize. We give symbols to phenomenon that arise from a multitude of particulars holding a similar quality. For example, when it rains, we look to explain it by relating the particular instances of raining to each other. And in fact, we go further; other phenomena described in science may be generalized to apply to explain rain. So, the possibility of universalization seems to part of our own conception of a general order of existence. This belief is clothed with an aura of factuality in that we (or at least most) do seem to believe in science, for example. Although I can conceive of the Cartesian possibility that science is wrong and pure chance is the cause for why technology, the product of science, works, I cannot bring myself to believe it. Thus, the way I understand the world seems uniquely real to me, and so I act in response to this worldview in a way that is uniquely realistic.

The congruence with Geertz and this loose notion of our worldview is, funnily enough, suggested in Asad’s critique: “one consequence of [Geertz’s] view would in principle render any philosophy that [attempts to come positively to terms with the human condition] into religion (to the annoyance of the nineteenth-century rationalist” (Asad 46). That is, perhaps Geertz’s should have claimed to be defining a worldview. However, because the similarities between Geertz’s definition of religion and a post-Enlightenment worldview he seems to be working out of are so great, they affirm Asad’s qualification that we maybe should not try to apply them to different religions or worldviews. The tendency to try to universalize is in fact particular to the moment in history. What this really points out, though, is the inescapability of our own existing worldviews when trying describe the world. For example, in defining a religion, we can point out how our own history of post-Enlightenment has affected our conception of religion. But to escape or transcend this history is to either remove the content of this particular example of worldview from our form of religion, or to argue that accounting for our history allows us to become more objective.

In the first case, if we were to truly succeed in removing all the particular content of religion from a form of religion, we would be reduced to ‘symbol pushing’, discussing semantics devoid of meaning – the algebra of symbols. In the second case, we forget that the value judgment of objectivity draws from our own worldview. It is better to recognize the inescapability of working from within a worldview and acknowledge the limitations.From there, one can create definitions with the understanding that they are valid only within a certain way of thinking, and is useful for that way of thinking only. Thus, its historicity and its immediacy in use is recognized.

Certainly, I operate from within this worldview that believes that reason is useful and desirable. And it seems that Geertz, Asad, and I all strive toward the similar value of objectivity within our worldview. Ultimately, Asad raised many important points that highlights the limitations of Geertz’s theory that is useful within our shared values of objectivity. However, for the sake of consistency within our own worldview, we should also remember that we do work from within our worldview that limits what we do see and can understand. I think that now, we are better suited to define religion: first we must ask: to what purpose? And if to understand and challenge our own worldviews better, then maybe Geertz is fine enough a starting place.
